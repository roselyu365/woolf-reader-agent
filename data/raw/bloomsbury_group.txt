The Bloomsbury Group was a loose collective of British writers, intellectuals, philosophers, and artists active in the early twentieth century. Rather than a formally organized group, they were friends and relatives connected through Cambridge University and King's College London who gathered in the Bloomsbury district of London.

Core Membership: Ten individuals formed the heart of the group: Clive Bell, Vanessa Bell, E.M. Forster, Roger Fry, Duncan Grant, John Maynard Keynes, Desmond MacCarthy, Lytton Strachey, Leonard Woolf, and Virginia Woolf. Many were educated at Cambridge, where they encountered the Cambridge Apostles society and the philosopher G.E. Moore, whose ethical ideas deeply influenced their thinking.

Key Ideas and Values: The group embraced Moore's philosophy emphasizing "love, the creation and enjoyment of aesthetic experience and the pursuit of knowledge." They rejected Victorian bourgeois conventions in favor of personal relationships and individual pleasure. They championed modernist art and literature, with figures like Roger Fry introducing post-impressionism to British audiences through influential exhibitions in 1910 and 1912.

Virginia Woolf's Role: Virginia Woolf stood among the group's most significant figures, producing influential modernist novels and essays throughout her career. She published works addressing feminism and suffrage, and her personal relationships with fellow members—particularly her sister Vanessa—anchored the group's social life.

Legacy and Decline: The group's influence peaked during the 1920s but began fragmenting in the 1930s following deaths of key members and the approach of World War II.
