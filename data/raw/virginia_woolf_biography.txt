Virginia Woolf was born Adeline Virginia Stephen on January 25, 1882, in South Kensington, London, to an intellectually prominent family. Her father Leslie Stephen was a writer and historian, while her mother Julia Prinsep Jackson was a philanthropist. Woolf grew up in a blended household of eight children, including her sister Vanessa Bell, who became a celebrated painter.

Woolf received her education primarily at home through access to her father's extensive library rather than formal schooling, which was uncommon for girls at the time. She attended lectures at King's College London and studied classics and history, where she encountered early advocates for women's education and rights.

The deaths of close family members profoundly affected her mental health. Her mother's death in 1895 triggered her first documented psychological crisis. The death of her father in 1904 caused a severe breakdown during which she attempted suicide. Her brother Thoby's death in 1906 from typhoid fever marked what she later described as a "decade of deaths" that defined her adolescence.

After her father's death, Woolf and her siblings moved to Bloomsbury, becoming central to the influential Bloomsbury Group, an intellectual circle of writers and artists. She married Leonard Woolf in 1912, and together they established the Hogarth Press in 1917, which published much of her innovative work.

Woolf's major novels include Mrs Dalloway (1925), To the Lighthouse (1927), and Orlando (1928). She pioneered the literary device of stream of consciousness narration. Her essay "A Room of One's Own" (1929) became foundational to feminist literary criticism, arguing that women need economic independence and private space to create art.

Throughout her life, Woolf struggled with recurring mental illness, experiencing episodes of depression, insomnia, and what modern scholars attribute to bipolar disorder. She recognized that writing helped her manage her psychological struggles, stating that working kept her "afloat" during difficult periods.

Woolf's relationship with writer Vita Sackville-West, whom she met in 1922, proved creatively fruitful and personally meaningful. The two remained close friends until Woolf's death.

On March 28, 1941, Woolf drowned herself in the River Ouse near her home in Rodmell, Sussex, placing a stone in her pocket. She left a suicide note to Leonard expressing her conviction that she was "going mad again" and couldn't face another mental health crisis. Her final novel, Between the Acts, was published posthumously in 1941.
